01 — East Africa

Rwanda

Food Culture

Rwanda — the Land of a Thousand Hills — has a cuisine rooted in its fertile volcanic soil. Cassava, sweet potatoes, beans, sorghum, and plantains are the cornerstones. Rwandan cooking is humble, nourishing, and communal — meals are shared from a central dish and the kitchen is a place of warmth and gathering.

Isombe Brochettes Ibihaza
02 — Southern Africa

South Africa

Food Culture

South African cuisine is one of the world's most diverse — a layered history of Zulu, Xhosa, Cape Malay, Afrikaner, and Indian traditions on one plate. The braai unifies all communities around an open fire — Heritage Day (September 24) is unofficially National Braai Day. The Cape Malay community contributed the nation's fragrant spiced stews including bobotie and bredie.

Braai Bobotie Bunny Chow
03 — Horn of Africa

Somalia

Food Culture

Somali cuisine is shaped by the Horn of Africa's position at the crossroads of ancient Indian Ocean trade routes. The hallmark spice blend xawaash defines nearly every dish — warm and aromatic rather than hot. Rice dishes hold a central place, and camel is the most prized meat. Canjeero (fermented flatbread) and sabaayad (layered chapati) are served at every meal.

Suqaar Bariis Iskukaris Canjeero
04 — West Africa

Ghana

Food Culture

Ghana's cuisine is bold, complex, and unapologetically flavourful — built on plantain, cassava, yam, and groundnuts. Street food thrives: kelewele vendors in the evening, waakye in the morning. Eating is communal — meals are shared from a central bowl and eating with your hands is not only accepted but preferred. Fufu with groundnut soup is the quintessential Ghanaian dish.
